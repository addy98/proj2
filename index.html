Bubble Shooting         

"It feels like a hooper's gym."

Why shooting percentages are up in the NBA bubble

Since resuming play in Orlando this summer, many teams in the NBA have seen an uptick in offensive productivity, with significant increases in 3-point percentages as well as free-throw percentages.
This higher efficiency has been attributed to a number of different possible factors, and is notable because statistics from the past pointed to the opposite happening after a midseason break.
In fact, a number of players have exploded in the bubble, with Devin Booker of the Phoenix Suns commenting, "It feels like a hooper's gym," and TJ Warren of the Indiana Pacers playing like an MVP out of nowhere.
What does a pandemic have to do with hoopers playing out of their minds?
Let's find out.
arrow_downward

Playing Environment

In the Orlando bubble, every game is on a similar neutral court, and many players have enjoyed the consistency. Playing in the same environment allows players to get comfortable with the court and lighting and such. Additionally, benches are farther from the sideline, giving players taking corner threes more space to take a more relaxed shot, thus the higher 3 point percentage.

Time Off

The long late season break caused by the pandemic gave players a long rest coming into the playoffs - they usually have just finished an 82 game season. This could definitely have had a part in why offenses are operating more efficiently. Furthermore, a long break from team practices allows defenses to get out of sync more than offenses. Defensive chemistry is harder to cultivate and is more easily lost, and this could also contribute to more offensive firepower.

No Fans

In addition to the obvious lack of fan noise and heckling, there is no fan seating to light. All the light is focused on the court, so the net is easier to see for players. Tim Hardaway Jr., a guard known for shooting threes, noted that "You can't see through the backboard, shooting-wise, since it's blacked out."

Depth Perception

This term has been thrown around a lot in the bubble. Normal NBA arenas are shaped like a stadium - they flare out as you go up in the stands so as many spectators as possible can see the game. This isn't the case in the bubble - theres the court, and then it's just four vertical walls, which more closely resembles the depth perception players are used to in their practice facilities.

Why This is Notable

Three point percentage typically goes down in the playoffs but the this year it is up. Because of the the style of play of today's NBA and the boost in accuracy in the bubble, players who favor shooting have seen tremondous upswings in their offensive productivity. The most notable example is Jamal Murray of the Denver Nuggets. In the bubble playoffs, he is shooting 51% from the field and 46% from three, all on top of averaging 26.9 points per game.
